Chit Funds

Chit funds are a traditional Indian group-savings scheme — part savings, part borrowing, part investment. This page tracks your subscriptions, records each monthly installment, works out the dividend you earned, and estimates your annualised return (XIRR) so you can see whether a chit is actually paying off.

Open Chit Funds → Register a chit →

New to chit funds? Start here

A chit fund is a group of people (say 25 members) who agree to pool money every month for a fixed number of months. Here's the cycle in plain terms:

  • A foreman (the organiser) runs the scheme. The total monthly pool is the chit value — e.g. 25 members × ₹20,000 = ₹5,00,000.
  • Each month every member pays a monthly installment into the pot.
  • That month, members bid in an auction for the pot. Whoever is willing to take the least money (i.e. accept the biggest discount, usually because they need cash) wins the pot for that month.
  • The discount the winner gave up (minus the foreman's commission) is shared back to every member as a dividend — so in months you don't win, you still get a little money back, which lowers your effective outgo.
  • Once you've won, you keep paying your installments until the scheme ends, but you can't bid again.

So a chit is a savings plan if you win late (you accumulate, then collect a lump sum), and a loan if you win early (you get a big sum up front and repay it over the remaining months). The dividends are your "interest earned" along the way.

The key number: what you actually pay each month is the installment minus the dividend. That net figure — added up across all months — is your real cost, and it's what the app uses to judge your return.

Registering a chit

Two ways to set one up:

  1. Register manually. Click Register chit and enter the foreman, scheme name, chit value, monthly installment and duration (number of months).
  2. Import a Form XIV PDF. Click Import from PDF and upload the chit ledger statement (the "Form XIV" your foreman issues). The app reads the scheme details and your installment history straight off the document.

Importing saves the most time. A Form XIV ledger already lists every installment you've paid and the dividend on each — importing it back-fills your whole history in one go instead of recording months one by one.

Recording a monthly installment

Each month, log what you paid. On the list page, chits due in the next 30 days appear in a Due in next 30 days panel with a Pay installment button (the quick-pay modal); you can also record from the chit's detail page.

  1. Open the quick-pay modal — it shows the month number and the monthly installment for reference.
  2. Enter the amount you actually paid (this is your net cheque after the dividend was adjusted).
  3. The dividend auto-calculates as installment − amount paid. Pick the date and payment method, then record.

Don't double-subtract the dividend. The amount you type is the net you paid. The app derives the dividend from it (dividend = monthly installment − amount paid). If you've already netted the dividend off in your head, just enter the cheque amount — don't subtract it again.

Months are 1-indexed. Month 1 is the chit's start date, month 2 is one month later, and so on. The app counts installments from the start month, not from zero.

Marking a chit as WON

When you win the auction and collect the pot, mark the chit WON from its detail page and record the amount received (your winning cheque after the foreman's discount/commission). The chit's status badge turns green, and the winnings show up in your totals. You keep recording installments afterwards — a won chit still runs to the end of its term.

XIRR — is this chit worth it?

XIRR (annualised internal rate of return) turns a messy stream of payments and receipts into a single yearly % you can compare against an FD or a mutual fund. The app builds the cash flows from your chit's current state and recomputes XIRR automatically as you record installments and winnings.

The estimate is deliberately conservative:

  • Past months use your actual net outgo.
  • Future months assume the full installment with no dividend — a worst-case assumption.
  • If you haven't won yet, it assumes the worst-case win timing (winning at the very end), which understates rather than overstates your return.

Read XIRR as a floor, not a forecast. Because future dividends are ignored and a late win is assumed, your real return is usually a bit better than the number shown. A positive XIRR on an un-won chit is a genuinely good sign.

The list page also shows a Portfolio XIRR — your chits' returns weighted by net outgo — alongside totals for dividends and winnings received.

Chit funds & your net worth

Your net contribution across every chit — what you've put in, net of dividends — counts toward your net worth, including chits you've already WON (you're still on the hook for remaining installments, so the contribution is real). Keeping installments up to date keeps this figure accurate.

Tips & gotchas

  • Enter the cheque, not the net. The amount-paid field is your actual payment; the app computes the dividend — never pre-subtract it.
  • Month 1 = start date. If your installment numbers look off by one, check the chit's start date.
  • Two chits, same name? If a payment lands on the wrong chit, look for a duplicate scheme name — near-identical chits are easy to mix up. Rename one to tell them apart.
  • XIRR is conservative by design. Don't be alarmed by a modest figure on a chit you expect to win early — the estimate assumes the worst case.
  • Record dividends as they happen. Logging each month keeps both your net worth and XIRR honest; back-fill via the Form XIV import if you've fallen behind.
  • Deleting a chit removes its history. The installment ledger is deleted with the chit.
