Real Estate

Track every property you own — its current value feeds your net worth, its rent shows up as income, and any home loan against it is netted off to reveal your true equity.

Open Real Estate →

The summary at a glance

The list page opens with four figures across the top:

StatWhat it means
Total Notional ValueYour current valuations added up — what the bricks are worth today
Total Loan OutstandingMortgage balances still owed across all properties
Total Monthly RentCombined rent from properties you've marked as rented
Net EquityNotional value − loans = the slice that's genuinely yours

Net worth uses the full valuation, not equity. The home dashboard counts a property's valuation as an asset and counts the loan separately as a liability. The result is the same bottom line — this page just shows the two sides pre-netted as “Net Equity”.

Adding a property

  1. Open Real Estate → Add property.
  2. Pick the type: Residential (apartment, independent house), Commercial (shop, office, godown), Land (agricultural or other), or Plot (residential/commercial plot).
  3. Fill in name, address, city, state, pincode and area (sqft).
  4. Enter the purchase date and purchase price, then the current notional value and the date you valued it. Leave the current value blank and it falls back to your purchase price.
  5. If it earns rent, mark it rented and enter the monthly rent.
  6. Save. A card appears showing the valuation, your gain/loss since purchase, and any rent.

Keeping the valuation current

Property doesn't have a live market price the way a stock does, so you keep the valuation honest. Open a property and use Edit → Save to update the current notional value and the valuation date whenever you get a fresh estimate — a broker quote, a circle-rate revision, or a nearby sale.

Each card shows a “Last valued N months ago” badge. Once a valuation is more than six months old the badge turns amber as a nudge — a stale property value is the most common reason a net-worth figure drifts from reality. Update it a couple of times a year.

Rental income

Mark a property as rented and enter its monthly rent, and a Rental income summary card appears at the bottom of the page with your combined monthly rent and its annualised total across all let properties. This rent also flows into your cashflow and planning views — and, at retirement, into the income mix if you keep the property.

The linked mortgage

If you bought with a home loan, record the outstanding mortgage on the property so the “Net Equity” math is right. For the loan itself — EMI, interest rate, amortisation schedule and the tax-deduction flags (Section 80C principal, Section 24(b) interest) — manage it under Liabilities. That's where the loan is subtracted from your net worth and where its interest feeds your tax workings.

Record the home loan in one place as your single source of truth — your liability record. The mortgage figure on the property is for the on-page “Net Equity” readout; the dashboard's net worth subtracts the liability record. Keeping the outstanding balance updated as you pay down the EMI keeps both views in sync.

Planning for retirement

Each property carries a retirement intent badge — For sale at retirement, Rental — kept, or Self-occupied. This tells the retirement planner whether to treat the property as a lump sum you'll liquidate, an ongoing rental income stream, or a home you'll keep and not monetise. Set it on the detail page so your retirement projection reflects your actual plan.

Removing a property

Sold or no longer relevant? Use the delete (trash) action on the card and confirm. This removes it from your net worth — there's no undo, so make sure the loan and any capital-gains record are sorted first if you sold it.
