Retirement Assets

These are your long-horizon, mostly fixed-income holdings — the slow-and-steady money that compounds quietly in the background. Each one is tracked on its own page, valued in ₹, and rolled into both your Net Worth and your retirement projections.

Five asset classes live here: the National Pension System (NPS), Employees' Provident Fund (EPF), Small Savings schemes (PPF, NSC, KVP, SSY, SCSS), Fixed Deposits, and Forex Deposits. They share a pattern: you record a balance (or a principal), the app values it today, and projections grow it forward at a sensible rate.

NPS — National Pension System

The NPS page tracks your Tier-I and Tier-II accounts. For each account you record the account number, holder, PAN, tier, opening date, and the current fund value — optionally split across the Equity, Debt, and Alternative sleeves. The app shows your total value, your total contributed, and the resulting gain/loss.

What it tracks

FieldMeaning
TierTier-I is the locked-in retirement account; Tier-II is the flexible, withdraw-anytime add-on.
Total valueThe latest balance across all fund sleeves — this is the number that counts to net worth.
Total contributedWhat you (and optionally your employer) have put in, so the app can show gains.
Expected maturityThe date the account is expected to mature/annuitise (typically age 60).

How it's valued & feeds projections

NPS is a balance-tracked asset: the value you enter is taken as today's worth. There's no live NAV feed for your specific NPS scheme, so update the balance periodically (the CRA statement is the source of truth). In retirement projections, NPS grows forward at its asset-class rate and the corpus is treated partly as a lump sum and partly as an annuity that pays a taxable pension in your retirement years.

You can bulk-import account details from an NPS Statement of Transactions (SOT) PDF using the import button on the NPS page — saves typing the account number, PAN, and opening date by hand.

EPF — Employees' Provident Fund

The EPF page holds your provident-fund balances. Despite the name, this is also where PPF-style and VPF (Voluntary Provident Fund) balances live if you prefer to keep them grouped with EPF rather than under Small Savings. You record the establishment/account number, the current balance, and your contribution history.

How it's valued & feeds projections

Like NPS, EPF is balance-only — the figure you enter is today's value and counts to net worth as-is. EPF earns a government-declared rate each year; in projections the balance compounds forward at the provident-fund rate until your retirement age. Update the balance after each annual EPF interest credit (or whenever you pull a fresh passbook).

EPF and PPF contributions also qualify for Section 80C tax deductions. Record the contribution amounts so the Income Tax module can pick them up.

Small Savings — PPF, NSC, KVP, SSY, SCSS

The Small Savings page covers the government-backed deposit schemes, organised into tabs:

Each account carries its own scheme interest rate (e.g. PPF at one rate, SCSS at another), plus the balance and the interest earned to date. The header totals roll up the current balance and total interest for the tab you're viewing.

Per-instrument real rate vs class rate

By default, projections grow Small Savings at a single blended class rate. But these schemes each pay a different, known rate — so there's a per-instrument toggle in Settings that tells projections to use each account's own real interest rate instead of the one-size-fits-all class rate. Turn it on when your mix of schemes pays meaningfully different rates and you want the forecast to reflect that.

The real-rate option gives a more honest forecast: a 30-year PPF and a 5-year SCSS compound very differently. Set each account's rate accurately and flip the toggle on for the truest retirement number.

Fixed Deposits

The Fixed Deposits page tracks bank/company FDs. For each one you record the bank, principal, interest rate, maturity date, and an expected maturity amount. Every FD has a status: ACTIVE, MATURED, or BROKEN (closed early).

How it's valued & feeds net worth

  • Only ACTIVE principal counts to net worth. Once an FD matures or is broken, its principal drops out of your asset total — the money has moved into your bank account (or been reinvested elsewhere) and shouldn't be double-counted.
  • The page shows your total active principal, projected maturity value, and the implied interest you'll earn (maturity amount − principal).
  • A next-12-months maturity figure surfaces what's about to come due and convert into cash — handy for cashflow planning.

In projections, an FD's maturity appears as a one-time cashflow on its maturity date rather than as a perpetually-compounding asset — because an FD ends, it doesn't grow forever.

When an FD matures, mark its status MATURED so it leaves your net worth. If you roll the money into a new FD, add that as a fresh ACTIVE deposit.

Forex Deposits

The Forex Deposits page tracks balances you hold in a foreign currency — USD, AED, GBP, and so on. You record the amount in the native currency (not rupees), the bank, an optional interest rate, and a maturity date.

How it's valued

The ₹ value is live: each time the page loads, the app fetches the current <CCY>INR exchange rate and converts your foreign balance into rupees (rates are cached for about five minutes). That ₹ value is what flows into net worth.

If the live FX rate can't be resolved for a currency, the row shows "rate unavailable" rather than a misleading ₹0 — so a temporary feed hiccup never silently zeros out your net worth.

In projections, a forex deposit's maturity is emitted as a one-time INR event at the maturity date, converted at the rate in effect then — it isn't projected forward as a growing position.

How these roll up

All five classes feed the same two places:

  1. Net Worth — current values are summed into your total (active FD principal and live-converted forex included; matured FDs excluded).
  2. Retirement projections — balances compound forward at each asset class's rate; FDs and forex appear as one-time maturity cashflows; NPS contributes a taxable pension leg.

Refresh these balances a few times a year — at minimum after the annual EPF/PPF interest credit and whenever an FD matures. Stale balances are the most common reason a net-worth number looks "off".
